# The Philosophy of David

## The Book of Wisdom

*Being proverbs told through stories, for those who learn better from fools than from lectures*

---

### Chapter 1: On Living

1. And there was a man who said, "I shall begin living tomorrow."
2. And tomorrow came, and he said it again. And again.
3. And lo, he is still saying it, and his bones are in the earth, and his tomorrow never arrived.

---

4. A student asked, "Master, when should I start living fully?"
5. The master said, "You are dying right now. Your cells are dying. Your moments are dying. Your hair is probably falling out."
6. The student was alarmed. "Then I must hurry!"
7. "No," said the master. "You must slow down and notice that you are already living. The hurrying is what made you miss it."

---

8. There was a woman who saved her fine china for special occasions.
9. She died, and her children used the china once at the funeral, then sold it.
10. The moral: You are the special occasion. Eat your cereal from the fancy bowl.

---

11. A young man said, "I am not my thoughts."
12. His thoughts replied, "Yes you are. You definitely are. Also, remember that embarrassing thing from 2007?"
13. And the young man learned that knowing a truth and living it are different things, and both require practice.

---

14. And there was one who chased comfort, and found it, and sat in it.
15. And sitting in comfort, he became soft. And being soft, he became fearful. And being fearful, he sought more comfort.
16. And lo, he lived in a very nice prison of his own making, with excellent thread count sheets.

---

### Chapter 2: On Thinking

1. A man drew a map of his neighborhood and was very proud.
2. One day, his neighbor built a new fence.
3. The man looked at his map and said, "There is no fence there."
4. And he walked into the fence. And lo, his map did not protect his nose.

---

5. There was a wise man who was certain he was right.
6. And there was a fool who admitted he might be wrong.
7. Years later, the fool had learned many things.
8. The wise man had learned only how to argue louder.

---

9. A teacher asked, "Can two opposite things both be true?"
10. The student said, "Absolutely not."
11. The teacher said, "Is your mother both the kindest person you know and also occasionally very annoying?"
12. The student was silent, for he understood.

---

13. A philosopher spent thirty years perfecting his theory of truth.
14. His dog, who had spent thirty years napping and chasing squirrels, was considerably happier.
15. Both were correct in their own domain.

---

16. And there was a man who never changed his mind, for he believed this showed strength.
17. He also never changed his underwear, for the same reason.
18. His friends found both habits equally unpleasant.

---

19. A student said, "I think, therefore I am."
20. The master replied, "You also think at 3 AM that everyone hates you. How is that working out?"
21. The student learned to observe thoughts rather than believe them all.

---

### Chapter 3: On Others

1. A man was rude to a stranger on the street.
2. He did not know the stranger's mother had just died.
3. He did not know the stranger had not slept in three days.
4. He did not know anything, really. And that was the problem.

---

5. And there was a woman who kept a grudge for twenty years.
6. She polished it daily. She fed it her energy. She kept it warm at night.
7. Meanwhile, the one who wronged her had forgotten entirely and was having a very nice time in Florida.
8. The grudge did not inconvenience him at all. Only her.

---

9. A man said, "I will forgive when they apologize."
10. He is still waiting. His hair has turned white. The other person has moved to Portugal.
11. Forgiveness is not a transaction. It is setting down a weight that only you were carrying.

---

12. And there was one who talked much and listened little.
13. He said, "I have many friends!"
14. But he could not name their children, or their fears, or their joys.
15. He had many acquaintances. He had no friends.

---

16. A teacher said, "Before you correct someone, understand them."
17. The student said, "But they are clearly wrong!"
18. The teacher said, "Yes, and when has shouting that ever changed anyone's mind? Have you tried curiosity instead?"

---

19. And there were two who argued about politics.
20. Neither changed the other's mind. Both went home angry.
21. Their dogs, who had been waiting outside, were happy to see them regardless.
22. The dogs understood something the men did not.

---

### Chapter 4: On Adversity

1. A man stubbed his toe.
2. He then spent forty-five minutes explaining to anyone who would listen how unfair the furniture was.
3. The pain lasted ten seconds. The suffering lasted most of the afternoon.

---

4. A woman said, "Why do bad things happen to me?"
5. Her friend said, "Bad things happen to everyone. You are not special."
6. The woman was offended, then relieved. Being ordinary in one's suffering is strangely comforting.

---

7. And there was one who worried every day about what might go wrong.
8. Ninety percent of what he worried about never happened.
9. The ten percent that did happen, he handled fine anyway.
10. He spent years paying interest on debts that never came due.

---

11. A young tree grew in a place of no wind.
12. It grew tall and thin and proud.
13. Then a storm came, and it snapped.
14. The tree that had grown in the wind, bent and twisted, survived.
15. It was not pretty, but it was standing.

---

16. And there was a man who resisted every setback, crying, "This should not be!"
17. And there was another who said, "This is. Now what?"
18. The first man was very tired. The second was very busy solving problems.

---

19. A student complained, "I have failed three times."
20. The master said, "Wonderful. You now have three data points. What did they teach you?"
21. The student had not considered learning from failure. He had only considered being upset about it.

---

### Chapter 5: On Work

1. And there was a man who waited for motivation.
2. And he waited.
3. And lo, he is still waiting, and his garden is overgrown, and his neighbors have stopped asking about it.

---

4. A woman said, "I will write my book when inspiration strikes."
5. Inspiration struck twice in forty years.
6. She wrote two pages.
7. Her neighbor wrote one paragraph every day, inspired or not.
8. Her neighbor published six books. Neither was particularly inspired. But they were finished.

---

9. A craftsman made a perfect table.
10. It took him seven years.
11. His apprentice made forty tables in the same time. Some were crooked. All were sold.
12. The craftsman starved beautifully.

---

13. And there was one who said, "I will rest when the work is done."
14. The work was never done.
15. He did not rest. Then he rested permanently.
16. The work continued without him.

---

17. A student asked, "How do I find work I love?"
18. The master said, "Have you tried doing work, any work, excellently, and seeing if love grows?"
19. The student wanted a shortcut. There was no shortcut.

---

20. And there were two workers: one who focused on one thing, and one who did twelve things at once.
21. The one who focused finished.
22. The other had twelve almost-done things and a strong feeling of busy-ness.

---

### Chapter 6: On Meaning

1. A man searched for the meaning of life.
2. He looked under rocks and behind stars and in very expensive seminars.
3. He did not find it, because it was not there to be found.
4. It was there to be made, and he had been looking instead of making.

---

5. A woman said, "The universe has no purpose! We are dust! Nothing matters!"
6. Then she made lunch for her children, who were hungry.
7. She had declared nothing matters, then immediately acted as if something did.
8. Perhaps her philosophy was less accurate than her instincts.

---

9. And there was one who asked, "What is the meaning of life?"
10. And another who asked, "What meaning am I creating today?"
11. The first was paralyzed. The second was busy.

---

12. A philosopher wrote: "Life is absurd."
13. Then he wrote another book, and taught students, and loved his family.
14. Life was absurd, but he kept doing it anyway.
15. Perhaps participating in absurdity is its own answer.

---

16. And there was a man who found no meaning in small things.
17. He waited for grand meaning, cosmic meaning, meaning with trumpets.
18. He missed his children's laughter, the taste of bread, the feeling of sun.
19. Meaning was all around him, but he had set his threshold too high to notice.

---

### Chapter 7: On Death

1. A man said, "I do not think about death. It is morbid."
2. He lived as if he had forever.
3. He did not have forever.
4. He used his time accordingly poorly.

---

5. And there was one who remembered death daily.
6. His friends said, "How depressing!"
7. But he was not depressed. He was clear.
8. He said "I love you" more often. He held fewer grudges. His emails were shorter.
9. Death had taught him what mattered.

---

10. A dying man was asked, "What do you regret?"
11. He did not say: "I wish I had worked more weekends."
12. He did not say: "I wish I had checked email more often."
13. He said the things that all dying men say, and the living heard, and then forgot, and then learned again on their own deathbeds.

---

14. And there was a woman who was afraid of being forgotten.
15. She built monuments. She wrote her name on things.
16. She was forgotten anyway, like everyone else.
17. This was not tragedy. This was freedom. No one was watching anymore. She could have just lived.

---

18. A student asked, "What should I do with my one wild and precious life?"
19. The master said, "First, accept that it is wild, and precious, and one. Then do anything. But do it knowing those three things."

---

### Chapter 8: On Joy

1. A man chased happiness.
2. Happiness ran faster.
3. He chased harder. Happiness hid behind a tree.
4. Finally, exhausted, he sat down and did nothing.
5. Happiness, curious, came to sit beside him.

---

6. And there was one who was very grateful.
7. Her life was not perfect. Her roof leaked. Her car was unreliable.
8. But every day she found three things to appreciate.
9. Her happiness had nothing to do with her circumstances and everything to do with her attention.

---

10. A wise man said, "Comparison is the thief of joy."
11. Then he checked what his neighbor was driving, and his joy was stolen.
12. Knowing a truth and living it are different achievements.

---

13. And there were two: one who laughed at hardship, and one who cursed it.
14. Both faced the same hardship.
15. One was more pleasant to be around.

---

16. A student asked, "Is it wrong to feel joy when there is so much suffering in the world?"
17. The master said, "Is the world helped by your refusing to feel joy?"
18. The student thought. "No."
19. "Then perhaps your joy, shared, might help. And your guilt helps no one at all."

---

20. And there was one who sought joy in achievements, and found only brief satisfaction.
21. And one who sought joy in possessions, and found only brief pleasure.
22. And one who sought joy in presence, and found it everywhere.
23. The third was suspected of being simple. He did not mind.

---

*Here end the proverbs. Go forth and be wisely foolish, or foolishly wise. The distinction is smaller than you think.*
