# The Philosophy of David

## The Book of Good and Evil

*Being an Account of Cooperation, Dysfunction, and Why Nobody Can Just Get Along*

---

### Chapter 1: The Parable of the Ambitious Cell

1. In the days when the body was young, there lived a cell named Reginald in the kingdom of the Liver.

2. And Reginald did his work, filtering toxins and synthesizing proteins, as was the way of his people.

3. But one day Reginald looked upon his labor and said: "Why do I toil for a body I have never seen? Who is this Body that demands my service?"

4. And a neighboring cell named Martha said unto him: "The Body is all of us, Reginald. We are the Body."

5. But Reginald laughed. "You speak in riddles, Martha. I see only cells. I see only work. I see only my small corner of this wet darkness."

6. "I have decided," declared Reginald, "to do my own thing."

7. And Martha was troubled. "What is your own thing, Reginald?"

8. "Growth," said Reginald. "Multiplication. I shall divide and divide and become mighty. I shall be a kingdom unto myself."

9. And Martha said: "But we are already part of a kingdom. The nutrients we receive, the oxygen that feeds us—these come from the Body's cooperation."

10. "Fairy tales," said Reginald. "I make my own oxygen." (He did not. He was confused about basic biology.)

11. And so Reginald began to divide. And divide. And divide again.

12. His children filled the spaces between cells. They pushed against their neighbors. They demanded more blood, more sugar, more everything.

13. And the other cells cried out: "Reginald! You are crushing us! You are stealing our resources!"

14. But Reginald, now a great mass of Reginalds, said: "I am simply succeeding. Is this not what success looks like? I am the biggest. I am the most."

15. And for a time, the Mass of Reginald grew mighty.

16. But then a strange thing happened.

17. The Body grew weak. The blood grew thin. The oxygen grew scarce.

18. And the Mass of Reginald began to starve.

19. "What is this?" cried the Mass. "Why does my abundance become scarcity?"

20. And with its last whisper of consciousness, the Body spoke:

21. "You have killed your host, O Reginald. And in killing me, you have killed yourself. Did you think you could prosper while I perished?"

22. And Reginald, in the darkness of death, finally understood.

23. But understanding came too late, as it often does for those who learn only from consequences.

24. Thus ended the story of Reginald, who wanted to be free from the tyranny of cooperation.

25. He achieved his freedom. It lasted nearly eight months.

### Chapter 2: The Chronicles of Getmine, the City of Rational Self-Interest

1. In the land between two rivers, there arose a city called Getmine.

2. And the founders of Getmine were philosophers who had read too many books and experienced too few consequences.

3. They declared: "We shall build a society based on pure self-interest! Every person shall pursue only their own good, and by the magic of invisible hands, prosperity shall emerge!"

4. And there was great celebration, and there was a signing of documents, and there were many handshakes (which would soon be discontinued, as they helped others too much).

5. In the first year, the markets thrived. Each merchant seeking profit, each buyer seeking bargains, and lo, goods flowed like water.

6. "See!" cried the philosophers. "Self-interest works! We have cracked the code of society!"

7. But in the second year, the merchants realized they could profit more by lying about their goods.

8. And the buyers, being also self-interested, began to steal.

9. And the guards, being also self-interested, took bribes from both.

10. "We need laws!" cried the people. But the lawmakers, being self-interested, sold the laws to the highest bidder.

11. In the third year, families began to dissolve.

12. For why should a parent sacrifice for a child, when sacrifice is cost without immediate return?

13. And why should a child care for an aging parent, when the old produce nothing and consume much?

14. The orphanages filled. The elders wandered the streets. And everyone agreed this was efficient.

15. In the fourth year, education collapsed.

16. For no teacher would teach without payment exceeding what students could afford.

17. And no student would study, for the effort exceeded the immediate reward.

18. "Knowledge is for suckers," became the city's motto.

19. In the fifth year, the bridges began to fall.

20. For the engineers, being self-interested, had pocketed the money for quality materials.

21. And the inspectors, being self-interested, had approved the bridges for a modest fee.

22. And when the bridges fell, the lawyers grew rich suing everyone, and everyone grew poor being sued.

23. In the sixth year, the city had a visitor.

24. He was a merchant from a distant land, and he looked upon Getmine with confusion.

25. "Where are your roads?" he asked, for the roads had crumbled. (Road repair benefited everyone, which meant no one would pay for it.)

26. "Where are your hospitals?" he asked, for the hospitals had closed. (Sick people are bad investments.)

27. "Where are your children's laughter?" he asked, for the children had learned to trust no one.

28. The last philosopher of Getmine, now old and starving, approached the visitor.

29. "We did everything right," he whimpered. "Every person pursued their own good. Why did we not prosper?"

30. The visitor looked upon the ruins and said:

31. "You pursued your own good. But you forgot that your own good is tangled up with everyone else's good."

32. "You are not atoms bouncing in a void. You are threads in a cloth. When you pulled your thread free, the cloth unraveled."

33. "And now you are not a thread in a cloth. You are a thread on the ground. You are free. You are useless."

34. The philosopher wept, for he finally understood that his philosophy had been excellent at describing humans who did not need each other.

35. Unfortunately, no such humans existed.

36. The city of Getmine became a ruin, and travelers who passed by would say: "Here lies the civilization that proved selfishness works—for approximately six years."

### Chapter 3: The Great Debate at the Crossroads of Being and Breakfast

1. At the crossroads where all ideas meet, three travelers gathered one morning to settle the oldest question.

2. The first was a Priest, who carried tablets of divine law and a heavy sense of moral certainty.

3. The second was a Philosopher, who carried many books and a headache from reading them.

4. The third was a Goat, who carried nothing and wanted only to eat the Philosopher's books.

5. "I shall prove," declared the Priest, "that morality comes from God. Without the divine command, all is permitted, and chaos reigns."

6. "I shall prove," declared the Philosopher, "that morality comes from reason. The categorical imperative needs no deity."

7. The Goat said: "Maaah."

8. "Let us conduct an experiment," said the Priest. "Imagine a world without God. What prevents a man from murdering his neighbor?"

9. The Philosopher stroked his beard. "The social contract. The mutual benefit of non-murder. The—"

10. "Maaah," interrupted the Goat, and ate three pages of Kant.

11. "You see?" said the Priest. "Without divine law, even the beasts consume our wisdom."

12. "The Goat has no morality," conceded the Philosopher. "But observe: the Goat does not murder us. It merely eats paper."

13. "Because it lacks the intelligence for murder!" said the Priest.

14. "Or," said the Philosopher, "because murder does not serve its interests."

15. At that moment, a Wolf appeared from the forest.

16. And the Priest said: "Behold! The Wolf knows not God's law! It will surely destroy us!"

17. But the Wolf sat down and spoke (for this is a parable, and in parables, wolves speak):

18. "Destroy you? Why would I destroy you? I am a wolf. I need a pack to survive. Murder within the pack is suicide with extra steps."

19. "But you would kill the Goat?" asked the Philosopher, concerned for his remaining books.

20. "Only if I were hungry and the Goat were not part of my pack," said the Wolf. "Killing is costly. Fighting risks injury. I prefer cooperation when possible."

21. "But this is mere calculation!" cried the Priest. "This is not true morality! True morality comes from God!"

22. The Wolf scratched behind its ear. "Tell me, Priest. Why does your God want you to be good?"

23. "Because... because goodness is holy. Because we must serve the divine plan."

24. "And what is the divine plan?"

25. "The flourishing of creation. The harmony of all beings. The—"

26. "So," said the Wolf, "your God wants cooperation and flourishing. And I want cooperation and flourishing. We have arrived at the same place from different paths. Does it matter which path we took?"

27. The Priest sputtered. The Philosopher took notes. The Goat ate the notes.

28. "Consider this," said the Wolf. "Your cells do not believe in God. They do not read philosophy. Yet they cooperate magnificently."

29. "They have no choice!" said the Priest.

30. "Do they not? Some cells choose to defect. You call them cancer. They pursue their own good without regard for the body. They always die. The structure of reality punishes them, no deity required."

31. "So morality is just... survival?" asked the Philosopher.

32. "Morality is the shape of successful cooperation," said the Wolf. "It is not arbitrary. It is not invented. It is discovered, like mathematics."

33. "Two plus two equals four whether or not God decrees it. And cooperation beats defection whether or not God commands it."

34. The Priest sat heavily upon a rock. "Then what need have we of God?"

35. "Comfort, perhaps. Community. Story. Meaning. These are good things," said the Wolf. "But they are not the source of morality. They are fellow travelers."

36. The Philosopher closed his one remaining book. "So the Goat, with no reason and no God, does not murder us because..."

37. "Because the Goat is part of a system that does not reward goat-on-philosopher violence," said the Wolf. "The same system, whether you call it God's design or evolutionary logic or the mathematics of cooperation, produces the same result."

38. "Maaah," said the Goat, approvingly.

39. And the three travelers (plus Goat) continued their journey together, for they had discovered that whether morality came from above or from the structure of existence itself, the destination was the same.

40. Though the Philosopher had to buy new books, and the Priest had to reconsider his smugness, and the Wolf had to explain why it was not going to eat anyone.

41. The Goat had no regrets. The Goat never had regrets.

### Chapter 4: The Tragedy of King Consumus the Short-Sighted

1. In the kingdom of Plenty, there arose a king named Consumus, who believed himself very clever.

2. For Consumus had realized a great truth: "I am the King, and the kingdom exists to serve me."

3. And his advisors said: "Yes, Majesty, but also you exist to serve the kingdom, for without the kingdom—"

4. "Silence!" cried Consumus. "I weary of this talk of mutual obligation. I am extracting value. This is called good management."

5. In the first year, Consumus raised taxes to build himself a golden palace.

6. The people grumbled, but they paid, for they loved their kingdom.

7. In the second year, Consumus raised taxes again to fill his palace with treasures.

8. The merchants began to leave, for business was no longer profitable.

9. "No matter," said Consumus. "There are always more merchants."

10. In the third year, Consumus raised taxes once more to build a second palace, for the first had become boring.

11. The farmers began to hide their grain, for why should they grow food only to have it taken?

12. "I shall send soldiers to find the grain!" declared Consumus. And he did.

13. But the soldiers needed to be paid, and so taxes rose again.

14. In the fourth year, the kingdom began to fray.

15. The young fled to other lands. The old died quietly. The middle-aged stopped having children, for what future could they offer?

16. Consumus sat in his golden palace and noticed something strange.

17. "Why are my taxes collecting less gold?" he demanded.

18. "Because, Majesty," said his last remaining advisor, "there are fewer people to tax."

19. "Then tax them more!"

20. "We cannot, Majesty. They have nothing left."

21. Consumus was confused. "But I have two palaces full of gold. How can the kingdom be poor?"

22. "The gold in your palace came from the kingdom," explained the advisor. "When you moved it from the kingdom to your palace, the kingdom became poorer."

23. "But I am the kingdom!" shouted Consumus.

24. "No, Majesty. You are the head of the kingdom. The kingdom is the body. And you have been drinking the body's blood."

25. In the fifth year, the neighboring kingdoms, sensing weakness, began to circle.

26. Consumus tried to raise an army, but the young men had fled or starved.

27. He tried to buy mercenaries, but discovered that his gold was worthless, for there was no economy left to give it value.

28. "What is happening?" wailed Consumus. "I did everything right! I maximized extraction! I optimized for personal gain!"

29. And the advisor, packing his bags to flee, turned and said:

30. "You confused the goose for the golden eggs. You ate the goose. Now there are no more eggs."

31. "You confused the tree for the fruit. You cut down the tree. Now there is no more fruit."

32. "You confused the system for the resources. You consumed the system. Now there is no more you."

33. Consumus died in his golden palace, surrounded by treasures he could not eat.

34. The neighboring kingdom, when it arrived, melted down his gold to build a hospital.

35. And thus ended the reign of Consumus, who proved that a parasite should never kill its host, and a king should never forget that he is also a servant.

36. His epitaph read: "Here lies one who took everything, and therefore had nothing."

### Chapter 5: The Fellowship of the Reluctant Cooperators

*Being a Collection of Lesser Parables on Why Jerks Eventually Learn to Play Nice*

**The Parable of the Two Farmers**

1. Two farmers lived side by side, and they hated each other with a passion reserved for neighbors.

2. The first farmer said: "I shall never help that fool. Let his crops rot."

3. The second farmer said: "I shall never help that idiot. Let his barn burn."

4. And for many years, they farmed alone, and their hatred sustained them.

5. But then came the Great Drought.

6. The first farmer's well ran dry, but the second farmer's well still flowed.

7. And the second farmer's harvest failed, but the first farmer's grain stores were full.

8. They stood at their shared fence, dying of thirst and hunger respectively.

9. "I would rather die than ask for your help," said the first.

10. "I would rather die than offer it," said the second.

11. They both nearly died.

12. Then, at the last moment, the first farmer's wife threw a bucket of grain over the fence.

13. And the second farmer's husband sent a barrel of water in return.

14. "Our spouses have betrayed us!" cried the farmers.

15. "Our spouses have saved you," replied the spouses. "Now shut up and cooperate."

16. And the farmers, grudgingly, began to trade. And then to talk. And then to share tools.

17. By the time they died, they were not friends—some hatreds run too deep—but they were alive.

18. And their children, who had no stake in the ancient feud, became friends, and their grandchildren married.

19. Thus cooperation emerged not from love, but from the mathematics of survival.

20. The universe does not require you to like your neighbors. It merely punishes you for ignoring them.

**The Parable of the Bacteria Who Discovered Virtue**

21. In the ancient seas, there lived bacteria who knew nothing of good and evil.

22. They simply consumed and divided, consumed and divided, as was their way.

23. Some bacteria developed a trick: they could poison their neighbors and steal their resources.

24. "This is clever," said these bacteria. "We are the fittest. We shall survive."

25. But then a strange thing happened.

26. Other bacteria developed a counter-trick: they began to share resources and protect each other.

27. "This is stupid," said the poisoners. "Why share when you can steal?"

28. Yet the sharers prospered. For when one sharer was attacked, others came to help. When resources were scarce, they divided them fairly.

29. And the poisoners, though mighty alone, found themselves surrounded by enemies.

30. Generation after generation, the sharers outbred the poisoners.

31. Not because they were virtuous—they had no virtue, being bacteria.

32. But because the strategy of cooperation was mathematically superior.

33. Eventually, some bacteria cooperated so thoroughly that they merged into a single organism with many cells.

34. And this organism was so successful that its descendants would one day write philosophy books and argue about morality.

35. Thus virtue was discovered before virtue was invented.

36. The math was always there. The feelings came later to motivate the math.

**The Parable of the Prisoner's Dilemma**

37. Two thieves were captured and placed in separate cells.

38. The jailer said to each: "If you betray your partner and they stay silent, you go free and they get ten years. If you both betray, you both get five years. If you both stay silent, you both get one year."

39. Each thief thought: "If I betray and they don't, I win. If I betray and they also betray, at least I'm not the sucker. Betrayal is always the smart choice."

40. Both betrayed. Both got five years.

41. In prison, they met a wise old convict who asked: "Are you thieves who will never meet again, or are you thieves who must work together tomorrow?"

42. "We were partners," they admitted. "We would have worked together again."

43. "Then you were fools," said the old convict. "One-time games reward betrayal. Repeated games reward cooperation."

44. "But how could we trust each other?" they asked.

45. "You build trust through time. You start by cooperating. You punish betrayal. You forgive return to cooperation. This is called Tit for Tat, and it beats every other strategy in the long run."

46. "But what if we never see each other again?"

47. "Then you should not be partners. Partnership requires a future. Without a future, there is no trust. Without trust, there is no partnership. Without partnership, you are alone. And alone, you are weak."

48. The thieves served their time and emerged wiser.

49. They never betrayed each other again, not because they had become good people, but because they had learned arithmetic.

50. Thus even criminals can discover morality, if the game is long enough.

### Chapter 6: The Meditation on Levels

*Being a Philosophical Interlude Disguised as a Conversation*

1. A student approached the teacher and said: "I understand that cooperation is good. But what happens when the levels conflict?"

2. The teacher said: "Tell me of your confusion."

3. "A cell that sacrifices itself for the body is good for the body. But is it good for the cell?"

4. The teacher smiled. "You have discovered the levels problem. Come, let us walk."

5. They walked through a forest, and the teacher pointed to an ant carrying a crumb.

6. "This ant will never reproduce. It works its whole life for the queen. Is the ant being exploited?"

7. "It seems so," said the student.

8. "But the ant's genes are in the queen. The ant reproduces through its sister. From the gene's perspective, the ant is succeeding magnificently."

9. "So the ant is good for the gene?"

10. "Yes. And the colony is good for the ant, in a way—it would die alone. And the forest is good for the colony, providing food and shelter. And the planet is good for the forest. Each level sustains the others."

11. "But what happens when they conflict? What if what's good for the colony is bad for the ant?"

12. "Then we must ask: can the colony thrive while the ants suffer? Usually not. A colony of suffering ants is a weak colony. The levels are linked."

13. "But sometimes a general must send soldiers to die. Is this not the higher level sacrificing the lower?"

14. "Sometimes, yes. And this is tragedy, not triumph. A good general weeps. A good society minimizes such choices. The best actions lift all levels; the worst destroy from within."

15. "But who decides which level matters most?"

16. "Ah," said the teacher, sitting on a rock. "This is where wisdom enters. There is no formula. There is only judgment, case by case, with incomplete information and uncertain consequences."

17. "This is unsatisfying," said the student.

18. "Reality often is," replied the teacher. "The universe did not consult our preferences when arranging itself."

19. "But surely there must be some principle!"

20. "There is. The principle is: if you sacrifice all the lower for the higher, the higher collapses. If you sacrifice all the higher for the lower, the lower cannot coordinate."

21. "The whole must serve the parts, as the parts must serve the whole. This is not balance for its own sake. This is structural necessity."

22. "But that means every choice is difficult!"

23. "No. Most choices are easy. Don't murder, don't steal, don't betray—these serve all levels. Only at the margins does conflict arise."

24. "And at the margins, be humble. You may be wrong. Act carefully, observe consequences, adjust."

25. The student sat in silence for a time.

26. "I think I understand," he said finally. "Good is not a fixed point. It's a direction. Toward cooperation, toward mutual thriving, toward all levels functioning."

27. "And evil?"

28. "Evil is the other direction. Toward dysfunction, toward consumption of the whole by the part or the part by the whole. Toward collapse."

29. The teacher nodded. "And neither requires a god to enforce them. They enforce themselves, eventually, through the mathematics of persistence."

30. "Patterns that cooperate endure. Patterns that consume themselves do not. This is not moral law handed down from above."

31. "It is engineering. The engineering of being."

32. The student rose and bowed. "I have one more question."

33. "Ask."

34. "If good is simply what helps patterns persist, isn't it... cold? Where is the love? Where is the meaning?"

35. The teacher laughed. "Love is one of the mechanisms by which patterns persist. Meaning is what conscious patterns create to motivate themselves toward persistence."

36. "The coldness you fear is an illusion. The math produces warmth. Cooperation feels good because it works. Love feels transcendent because it is transcendent—it transcends the individual toward the collective."

37. "You are not a machine grinding through optimization. You are a pattern that has developed feelings to guide it toward what works."

38. "The feelings are real. The meaning is real. They are no less real for having emerged from structure rather than descending from heaven."

39. The student smiled. "So I can love and cooperate not because I must, but because it is genuinely good?"

40. "Better: you can love and cooperate because you see that it is genuinely good, and therefore want to. The obligation and the desire point the same direction."

41. "This is not burden. This is freedom with purpose."

42. And the student went down from the mountain, and lived the rest of his days trying to lift all levels, knowing he would often fail, but understanding at last why the attempt mattered.

43. The teacher watched him go, and returned to the forest.

44. The ant was still carrying its crumb. The forest was still feeding the colony. The earth was still spinning through the void.

45. And all the levels, for one more day, held together.

---

*Here ends the Book of Good and Evil, which teaches that morality is not a set of arbitrary commands but the shape of successful existence, that cooperation emerges not from virtue but from necessity, that selfishness is self-destruction on delay, and that the universe, without anyone asking it to, rewards the patterns that work together and punishes those that tear themselves apart.*

*Go forth and be good—not because a god commands it, but because goodness is the path to persistence, and persistence is the only game in town.*
