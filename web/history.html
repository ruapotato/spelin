The Story of ·spelin

From George Bernard Shaw's dream of spelling reform to a modern phonemic alphabet for the digital age.

Part One

The Shavian Alphabet

George Bernard Shaw, the Nobel Prize-winning playwright, had a lifelong frustration with English spelling. He famously illustrated its absurdity with the word "ghoti"—which, using English spelling rules, could be pronounced "fish" (gh as in "rough," o as in "women," ti as in "nation").

"The English alphabet has only 26 letters to represent 40+ sounds. The result is chaos—a spelling system that has to be memorized word by word rather than learned by rules." — George Bernard Shaw

The 1958 Competition

When Shaw died in 1950, his will included provisions for a competition to create a new phonemic alphabet for English. The competition, held in 1958, was won by Kingsley Read with the "Shavian" alphabet—48 unique characters, one for each sound in English.

Shavian was elegant in its design: each letter was crafted for efficient handwriting, with tall letters for voiceless sounds and deep letters for voiced sounds. The alphabet was used to publish a special edition of Shaw's play "Androcles and the Lion" in 1962.

The Problem with Shavian

Despite its phonemic purity, Shavian never gained widespread adoption. The reasons became clear over time:

Designed for Handwriting

  • Optimized for pen-and-paper speed
  • Required learning 48 new symbols
  • Looked completely foreign
  • No connection to existing letters

Not Designed for Typing

  • Required special fonts
  • Couldn't be used in most text fields
  • Incompatible with standard keyboards
  • Pre-dated the digital age

Shaw designed his alphabet for a world of handwritten letters and printed books. He couldn't have anticipated a world where most writing happens on keyboards and screens.

Part Two

The Birth of ·spelin

In 2026, David Hamner came across discussions about alternative alphabets while watching a Rob Words video on YouTube. The comments section was filled with people lamenting English spelling while dismissing alternatives like Shavian as "impractical."

The criticism was valid: learning 48 new symbols, installing special fonts, and being unable to use the system in standard text fields were real barriers. But the underlying idea—one symbol per sound—was sound.

The Key Insight

The problem wasn't phonemic writing itself. The problem was that existing systems were designed for the wrong medium. Shavian optimized for handwriting speed when most modern writing is typed. It created entirely new symbols when a standard keyboard has unused potential.

"What if we kept Shaw's phonemic principle but designed for keyboards instead of pens? What if we used characters people already know?"

Design Principles

Readability First

Use familiar ASCII characters. Build on existing letter knowledge rather than replacing it.

Typability

Every symbol must be accessible on a standard keyboard. No special fonts or character maps.

Digital Native

Works in any text field—email, SMS, social media, code comments. If you can type, you can write spelin.

Phonemic Purity

Maintain Shaw's core insight: one symbol, one sound. No exceptions, no silent letters, no ambiguity.

Part Three

How ·spelin Improves on Shavian

Familiar Foundations

Where possible, spelin uses letters for their most common sounds: 'p' for /p/, 'b' for /b/, 't' for /t/, etc. Most consonants work exactly as English speakers expect.

Case as Information

In spelin, uppercase vowels represent their "long" sounds (their letter names), while lowercase vowels represent their "short" sounds:

Symbol Sound Example
A /eɪ/ day, make
a /æ/ cat, man
E /iː/ see, me
e /ɛ/ bed, said

Numbers for Extended Sounds

English has more vowel sounds than vowel letters. Spelin uses numbers for sounds that don't have natural letter equivalents:

Symbol Sound Example
1 /aʊ/ out, how
7 /ɜːr/ bird, her
8 /ð/ the, they
3 /θ/ think, math

The Namer Dot

Since spelin uses case for phonetic information, it can't use capitals for proper nouns. Instead, proper nouns are marked with a preceding middle dot (·):
